Defining Food and Nutrition Security

⏱️ 8 min
🎙️ NarrationAudio to be added · script ready
Narration script: Let's unpack this definition—every word matters. Understanding each component is essential for effective monitoring and intervention.
📖 FAO Definition
"Food and nutrition security exists when all people, at all times, have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food that meets their dietary needs and food preferences for an active and healthy life."

Let's unpack this definition—every word matters. Understanding each component is essential for effective monitoring and intervention.

👥 "All people"
Not just some. Not just those who can afford it. Every person, regardless of where they live, their economic status, or their social position. This emphasizes equity and inclusion.
⏰ "At all times"
Not just during good harvests or peaceful periods, but even during droughts, conflicts, or economic shocks. This speaks to resilience and stability.
🚪 "Physical, social, economic access"
Food might exist but be inaccessible due to poor roads (physical), cultural norms (social), or high prices (economic). All three barriers must be addressed.
🥗 "Sufficient, safe, nutritious"
Quantity alone isn't enough. Food must meet dietary needs and be free from contamination. A diet of only maize lacks essential nutrients children need.
💪 "Active and healthy life"
The ultimate goal isn't just survival, but thriving. Food security enables people to work, learn, and contribute to their communities.

🔑 Critical Distinction

Food Security refers to availability and access to food, while Nutrition Security ensures that food provides adequate nutrients and that people's bodies can absorb and use those nutrients effectively.

Example from Burundi: Maize might be available in markets (food security), but if children suffer from diarrhea due to unsafe water, preventing nutrient absorption, they experience nutrition insecurity even when food is present. Both dimensions must be addressed together.

The Global and EAC Context

⏱️ 10 min
🎙️ NarrationAudio to be added · script ready
Narration script: Let's examine the sobering reality of where we stand globally and in our region. Understanding the magnitude of the challenge is the first step toward solving it.

Let's examine the sobering reality of where we stand globally and in our region. Understanding the magnitude of the challenge is the first step toward solving it.

Global hunger has fallen from its 2021 peak — but remains above pre-pandemic levels

Globally, an estimated 673 million people faced hunger in 2024 — down from the 2021 peak of about 811 million, but still above pre-pandemic levels. Progress is uneven: hunger continues to rise in Africa, where the prevalence of undernourishment has now surpassed 20%. Child stunting has improved globally to 23.2% (150.2 million children) in 2024, yet remains far higher across much of the EAC.

The East African Community

Our region comprises seven countries: Burundi, Democratic Republic of Congo, Kenya, Rwanda, South Sudan, Tanzania, and Uganda. Together, we're home to over 343 million people—a population growing at 2.9% annually, one of the highest rates in the world.

Three Interconnected Drivers of Food Insecurity

💰 Poverty

With 34.9% of our population living below the international poverty line, millions cannot afford adequate food. In Uganda in 2022, when food prices spiked, families reported skipping meals or reducing portion sizes—the painful trade-offs of poverty.

⚔️ Conflict and Displacement

South Sudan's ongoing civil conflict has displaced millions, disrupting agricultural production and markets. In DRC, armed groups control key agricultural areas, preventing farmers from accessing their land. Conflict doesn't just destroy crops; it destroys entire food systems.

🌡️ Climate Change

Drought events have increased by 30% since 2000. Kenya's 2022 drought left 4 million people needing assistance. South Sudan's 2020 floods affected 1 million. The 2020 locust invasion in Kenya destroyed crops across 70,000 hectares. Our region faces increasingly unpredictable weather.

⚠️ These factors don't operate in isolation. A farmer experiencing drought (climate) may lose their livelihood (poverty), and in a country with weak governance, this can contribute to conflict over scarce resources. It's a vicious cycle—but cycles can be broken through evidence-based action.

Food insecurity isn't one-size-fits-all. Understanding the different types is essential because each requires different assessment approaches and interventions. The EAC Framework recognizes three main types:

Aspect Chronic Transitory Acute
Duration Long-term (years/generations) Short-term (weeks to months) Sudden (days to weeks)
Causes Structural: poverty, inequality, poor infrastructure Seasonal or temporary shocks: drought, price spikes Emergencies: conflict, disasters, economic collapse
Recovery Capacity Low without intervention Moderate; can bounce back Requires external assistance
EAC Example 🇧🇮 Burundi: 50% child anemia rate persisting for years 🇹🇿 Tanzania: 2022 drought affecting one harvest season 🇸🇸 South Sudan: 2023 crisis with 7M in Crisis phase
Response Needed Long-term development: agriculture, health, education Medium-term support: cash transfers, agricultural inputs Emergency aid: food distribution, therapeutic feeding
Assessment Type Comprehensive (DHS, Food Consumption Surveys, HDDS) Either, depending on severity Rapid (IPC, CARI, HEA)

⚠️ Critical Insight: Interconnection

These types are interconnected. Acute crises, if not addressed, can become chronic. When South Sudanese families sell their farming tools to buy food during an acute crisis, they lose the means to produce food next season—sliding into chronic insecurity.

Similarly, chronically food-insecure households are more vulnerable to shocks. A Burundian family already eating inadequate diets has no buffer when drought strikes—a transitory event quickly becomes acute for them.

Detailed Examples from the EAC

📘 Chronic Food Insecurity: Rural Burundi

In rural Burundi, where 50% of children suffer from anemia, this is chronic food insecurity. Families may produce enough calories from beans and cassava, but their diet lacks essential micronutrients—iron, vitamin A, zinc.

Year after year, generation after generation, malnutrition persists. Children grow up stunted, affecting their lifelong earning potential, which perpetuates the cycle.

→ Requires long-term solutions: improving agricultural productivity, strengthening health systems, enhancing education, building roads to markets, empowering women.

📙 Transitory Food Insecurity: Tanzania Drought

During Tanzania's 2022 drought, normally food-secure households suddenly faced crop failures. Maize prices spiked 30%. Families coped by reducing meal frequency, eating less preferred foods, even selling small livestock.

But when the rains returned and the next harvest came, most households recovered their food security.

→ Requires short-to-medium term responses: cash transfers, input subsidies to replant, water trucking. Goal: prevent temporary shocks from becoming chronic by depleting assets.

📕 Acute Food Insecurity: South Sudan Crisis

In 2023, IPC analysis classified 7 million people—over 60% of South Sudan's population—in Crisis (Phase 3) or worse levels of acute food insecurity. Conflict disrupted farming and markets. Floods destroyed crops. Hyperinflation made food unaffordable.

People were literally at risk of starvation.

→ Requires immediate humanitarian response: emergency food distributions, therapeutic feeding for malnourished children, protection of livelihoods to prevent total collapse.

Can we really make a difference?

The answer is an emphatic YES!

But only if our actions are guided by evidence

Food and nutrition security monitoring is the systematic collection, analysis, and use of data to understand:

  • Who is food insecure?
  • How many people are affected?
  • Where they are located?
  • Why they face insecurity?
  • What interventions will work?

Without this evidence, we're flying blind—wasting resources on ineffective programs while the truly vulnerable remain invisible.

The Story Behind the EAC Framework

Each EAC Partner State was conducting FNS assessments, but using different methodologies, collecting different indicators, at different times. Kenya used IPC tools for rapid assessment. Tanzania relied heavily on DHS surveys. Rwanda had strong nutrition surveillance. But the data wasn't comparable. Regional reporting was fragmented. Donors couldn't see the full picture.

The EAC Secretariat recognized that to achieve the vision of the Food and Nutrition Security Policy (adopted in 2016)—to attain FNS for all 343 million people of the EAC throughout their life cycle—we needed harmonization.

What the Framework Provides:

1
23 Core Indicators

Aligned with SDGs, CAADP, and WHO targets. Covering all four pillars: availability, access, utilization, and stability.

2
Standardized Methodologies

For comprehensive assessments (chronic FNS) using DHS, surveys, and surveillance systems, AND rapid assessments (acute FNS) using IPC, CARI, and HEA.

3
Institutional Frameworks

Coordination mechanisms so governments, NGOs, research institutions, international partners, and communities work together.

4
Data Management Guidelines

Ensuring the evidence collected actually informs policy and programs.

Your Role in This Framework

This course will equip you to implement this Framework. By Module 9, you'll be ready to conduct assessments, analyze data, coordinate stakeholders, and use evidence to drive action in your country and across the region.

🎯 Case Study: Four Access Dimensions

Economic Access Barrier - Kampala, Uganda (2024)

Market vendor Nakato saw maize meal prices rise from 3,000 to 4,800 UGX per kilo—a 60% increase. Her family's monthly income remained constant at 25,000 UGX.

Result: Previously buying 15 kg monthly, she could now afford only 9 kg. Access deteriorated by 40%. Family reduced from three to two daily meals.

Physical Access Barrier - Karamoja, Uganda

Nearest functioning market is 30-40 km away. Roads are unpaved and impassable during rains.

  • 6-8 hour walking journey
  • Cannot carry large quantities
  • Risk of insecurity on roads
  • Opportunity cost—days spent traveling cannot be spent farming

Social Access Barrier - Pastoralist Communities

In northern Kenya, cultural norms around food and gender create constraints. Women may be excluded from decisions about selling livestock—the primary asset.

Impact: Women's specific nutritional needs (pregnancy, lactation) may not be prioritized in food acquisition decisions.

Legal Access Barrier - Refugee Settlements

In settlements like Kakuma (Kenya) or Nakivale (Uganda), legal restrictions limit livelihood options:

  • Restricted movement outside settlements
  • Inability to legally own land
  • Limited work permits
  • Barriers to starting businesses

🍎 Pillar 3: UTILIZATION

Definition

Food utilization refers to the use of food through adequate diet, clean water, sanitation, and health care to reach a state of nutritional well-being where all physiological needs are met.

🔑 Key Questions Utilization Answers

  1. Is the food nutritious?
  2. Is it safe to eat?
  3. Can people's bodies absorb and use the nutrients?
  4. Does the care environment support good nutrition?

Critical Insight: You can eat adequate food yet suffer malnutrition if the food lacks essential nutrients, is contaminated, or your body cannot absorb nutrients due to disease.

The Nutrition Pathway: From Food to Health

Food Consumed

Adequate quantity and quality must be consumed

Can break if: Inadequate availability/access, poor dietary diversity, food taboos, intra-household inequalities

Food Safe

Food must be free from contamination

Can break if: Aflatoxin contamination (55% of Nairobi maize samples exceed safe levels), bacterial contamination, chemical residues

Nutrients Absorbed

Body must absorb nutrients from food

Can break if: Diarrheal diseases (children may have diarrhea 6-12 times annually in poor WASH areas), intestinal parasites, repeated infections

Nutrients Used Effectively

Body must utilize nutrients for growth, immunity, energy

Can break if: Malaria increases metabolic demands, HIV/AIDS increases requirements while reducing absorption, chronic inflammation

Care Practices Support Nutrition

Especially critical for infants and young children

Can break if: Poor breastfeeding, inappropriate complementary feeding, inadequate responsive feeding, lack of healthcare seeking

⏱️ Pillar 4: STABILITY

🔗 Pillar Interdependencies

🎯 Critical Insight

The four pillars are NOT independent. They're deeply interconnected. A problem in one pillar creates cascading effects in others.

EAC Framework Emphasis

"The complexity of the food security problem can be simplified by focusing on four distinct, but interrelated dimensions: food availability, access, utilization, and stability."

Key Pillar Interactions

Interconnection Web

AVAILABILITY ACCESS UTILIZATION STABILITY

All pillars influence and depend on each other

Interaction 1: Availability ↔ Access

How Availability Influences Access

Kenya Drought 2022 Example:

  • Maize availability fell 40%
  • Prices increased 60%
  • Even households with income faced access challenges
  • Severe food insecurity jumped from 15% (2019) to 28.1% (2024)

How Access Influences Availability

DRC Infrastructure Example:

  • Remote areas lack market access (physical access barrier)
  • Farmers reduce production—why grow surplus you can't sell?
  • Lower production (availability ↓) follows from access constraints
  • Result: 42.5% severe food insecurity reflects both poor availability AND poor access

📊 Uganda Paradox Explained

The Numbers:

  • Produces 125% of national food requirements (AVAILABILITY: Excellent)
  • Yet 14.9% face severe food insecurity (ACCESS: Failing)
  • And 22.0% are undernourished (ACCESS + UTILIZATION: Failing)

Why? Availability alone cannot ensure food security. The food exists but people cannot access it due to poverty (41.7% below poverty line), poor infrastructure, gender inequality, and market inefficiencies.

Interaction 2: Access ↔ Utilization

Access → Utilization Pathway

Economic access constraints force poor diet quality:

Burundi Example:

  • 20.9% severe food insecurity (access)
  • 64.9% poverty (access)
  • Households buy only cassava/beans
  • Adequate calories but micronutrient deficiencies
  • Result: 55.3% child stunting despite "food secure" in calories

SOFI 2025: "One-third of women and two-thirds of children consumed diets that were not sufficiently diverse, putting them at risk of inadequate intake of essential vitamins and minerals."

Utilization → Access Pathway

Poor health reduces income-earning capacity:

Tanzania Malaria Example:

  • Adult sick with malaria 7-10 days
  • Cannot work, loses income
  • Medical costs deplete savings (~50,000-100,000 TSH)
  • Cannot afford food purchases
  • Household food access deteriorates

Intergenerational Impact: Child malnutrition impairs cognitive development, limiting future earning potential. SOFI: "Stunted children earn 20% less as adults, perpetuating poverty across generations."

🔄 The Vicious Cycle

Poor access → Poor diet quality → Malnutrition → Reduced productivity → Lower income → Poorer access → Worsening malnutrition...

Breaking the Cycle: Rwanda's integrated programs (VUP cash transfers + nutrition education + kitchen gardens + community health workers + Girinka) improved both access AND utilization simultaneously, breaking the reinforcing negative cycle.

Interaction 3: Stability ↔ All Pillars

How Instability Undermines All Pillars

South Sudan Case Study - Multi-Pillar Collapse:

Conflict (stability shock) →

  • AVAILABILITY ↓: Displacement prevents farming, production near zero in conflict zones
  • ACCESS ↓: Market disruption, 63.1% severe food insecurity
  • UTILIZATION ↓: Overcrowded camps with poor water/sanitation, disease rampant
  • STABILITY: Never restored, perpetual crisis prevents recovery

Result: 30.0% child stunting despite ongoing food aid. All four pillars collapsed simultaneously.

🏆 Rwanda Success Story: Stability Enables All Pillars

1994: All four pillars collapsed (genocide, infrastructure destroyed, markets ceased, mass displacement, 50% stunting)

Reconstruction Approach (1994-2024):

  1. Phase 1 - Restore Security: End conflict, resettlement, rule of law (fundamental stability)
  2. Phase 2 - Rebuild All Pillars Simultaneously:
    • Availability: Agricultural intensification, irrigation, research
    • Access: Infrastructure (densest road network), social protection (VUP), health insurance (Mutuelle 90%+)
    • Utilization: Community health workers, nutrition programs, WASH (58% safe water), Girinka
    • Stability: Good governance, policy consistency, disaster risk management

Results (2000 → 2024):

  • Food production: +65%
  • Poverty: 45% → 38.2%
  • Stunting: 50% → 29.8% (most dramatic improvement in Africa)
  • Exclusive breastfeeding: 70% → 80.9%
  • Severe food insecurity: Declining (estimated <15%)

Lesson: Stability enables all other pillars. Without security and predictability, investments in agriculture, markets, or health don't yield results.

  • Markets supported, not destroyed
  • Recovery in 9 months
  • No dependency created

TYPE 1: Emergency/Rapid Assessments

📘 Example: Butaleja Floods Assessment, Uganda (2024)

Context: River overflow displaced thousands after heavy rains

72-Hour Assessment Timeline:

  • Day 1: Team mobilized, key informant interviews, initial household surveys (30 HH), MUAC screening (95 children)
  • Day 2: Additional surveys (50 HH), market assessment, health facility check, more MUAC (105 children)
  • Day 3: Data analysis, report writing, findings presentation

Key Findings:

  • 14,200 displaced (IPC Phase 4 - Emergency)
  • 8,500 affected non-displaced (IPC Phase 3 - Crisis)
  • 72% lost ALL food stocks
  • Markets functional but prices +35%
  • GAM 8.5% (approaching serious threshold)

Response Mobilized (Day 4): Emergency food rations, cash transfers, water purification, therapeutic feeding, emergency shelter - Total: $450,000

TYPE 2: Baseline Assessments

ℹ️ Note

⚠️ CRITICAL: No baseline = No impact evaluation possible

Without baseline data, you CANNOT prove your program caused improvements. Donors increasingly REQUIRE baseline for funding.

📘 Example: Kirehe Nutrition Program, Rwanda (2021-2026)

Program: 5-year integrated nutrition program, $12 million budget

Baseline Survey (Feb 2021):

  • Sample: 600 treatment households + 300 control households
  • Duration: 6 weeks
  • Cost: $85,000 (0.7% of total budget)

Key Baseline Findings:

  • Stunting: 42.3% (Target: 32.3% by Year 5)
  • Minimum Dietary Diversity: 18.4% (Target: >40%)
  • Improved water access: 68.3% (Target: >85%)

Impact Measurement: Difference-in-Differences analysis will compare treatment vs control changes to isolate program impact.

TYPE 3: Comprehensive Assessments

Large-scale, in-depth assessments used for national policy and strategic planning. Example: Kenya's 2023 national assessment covered all 47 counties with 3,250 households over 6 months ($500,000 budget).

TYPE 4: Monitoring Systems

📘 Example: Tanzania mVAM (Mobile Monitoring)

System: Monthly phone surveys of 1,200 households

Early Warning Success (April 2023):

  • Detected crisis 2 months before it would become severe
  • Alerts triggered when: Poor FCS >25%, maize prices +50%, crisis coping doubled
  • Early response prevented escalation
  • Saved $12M (early action $3M vs emergency $15M+)

TYPE 5: Impact Evaluations

📘 Example: Rwanda Girinka Program Impact Evaluation

Program: "One Cow Per Poor Family" - Give dairy cow to poor households

Evaluation: 1,200 treatment + 1,200 control HH, 3.5 years post-distribution

Impact Results:

  • Milk consumption: +4.1 days/week
  • Child stunting: -7.2 percentage points
  • Household income: +13,300 RWF/month (~$15)
  • Return on investment: 46% annually

Outcome: Program scaled from 50,000 to 365,000 cows based on proven impact

🎯 Integrated Phase Classification (IPC)

ℹ️ Note

WHAT IS IPC?

The Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC) is a standardized scale for classifying the severity of acute food insecurity. It provides a common language used globally.

  • ✓ Used in 70+ countries, including ALL EAC member states
  • ✓ Enables comparison over time and between areas
  • ✓ Guides humanitarian response (Phase 3+ triggers assistance)
  • ✓ Based on convergence of evidence across FOUR domains

The Five IPC Phases

Phase Name Key Characteristics
1 Minimal/None • Households meet basic food needs
• FCS >42
• GAM <5%
2 Stressed • Minimally able to meet food needs
• FCS 28.5-42
• GAM 5-10%
3 Crisis • Food consumption gaps present
• FCS <28.5 (Poor)
• GAM 10-15%
• 20-40% using crisis coping
4 Emergency • LARGE food consumption gaps
• Very high acute malnutrition
• GAM 15-30%
• 40-60% crisis coping
5 Catastrophe/Famine • Starvation, death evident
• GAM >30%
• CDR >2/10,000/day

Four Evidence Domains for IPC Classification

ℹ️ Note

⚠️ CRITICAL PRINCIPLE:

IPC requires convergence of evidence across FOUR domains. Single indicators are NOT sufficient!

Domain 1: FOOD CONSUMPTION - Are people eating enough quality food?

  • Food Consumption Score (FCS)
  • Household Dietary Diversity Score (HDDS)
  • Household Hunger Scale (HHS)

Domain 2: LIVELIHOODS - What are people doing to survive?

  • Coping Strategy Index (CSI)
  • Livelihood coping: Stress → Crisis → Emergency coping

Domain 3: NUTRITION - Are children malnourished?

  • Global Acute Malnutrition (GAM)
  • Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM)

Domain 4: MORTALITY - Are people dying at above-normal rates?

  • Crude Death Rate (CDR): Deaths per 10,000/day
  • Under-5 Death Rate (U5DR)

📈 Food Consumption Score (FCS)

ℹ️ Note

WHAT IS FCS?

The Food Consumption Score (FCS) is a composite indicator measuring dietary diversity, food frequency, and relative nutritional importance of different food groups consumed by a household over 7 days.

Developed by: World Food Programme (WFP)
Standard recall period: Last 7 days
Result: Numerical score (0-112) classified as Poor, Borderline, or Acceptable

The FCS Food Groups and Weights

Food Group Examples Weight Max Days Max Points
Main staples Maize, rice, wheat, sorghum, pasta, bread, cassava, potatoes 2 7 14
Pulses/legumes Beans, peas, lentils, groundnuts, soybeans 3 7 21
Vegetables Tomatoes, onions, spinach, kale, cabbage, carrots 1 7 7
Fruits Bananas, mangoes, oranges, papaya, watermelon 1 7 7
Meat/fish Beef, goat, chicken, pork, fish (fresh or dried) 4 7 28
Milk/dairy Fresh milk, powdered milk, yogurt, cheese 4 7 28
Sugar Sugar, honey, jam, sodas, sweet biscuits 0.5 7 3.5
Oil/fats Cooking oil, butter, margarine, ghee 0.5 7 3.5

How to Calculate FCS

ℹ️ Note

FCS Calculation Steps:
  1. Ask: "In the last 7 days, how many days did your household eat [food group]?" (0-7 days)
  2. Record: Number of days for each food group
  3. Multiply: Days × Weight for each food group
  4. Sum: Add all weighted values = FCS
  5. Classify: FCS into Poor/Borderline/Acceptable

Borderline 28.5 - 42 Marginally adequate consumption Monitor, may need assistance
Acceptable >42 Adequate food consumption Generally food secure

🍎 Household Dietary Diversity Score (HDDS)

ℹ️ Note

WHAT IS HDDS?

The Household Dietary Diversity Score (HDDS) is a simple count of different food groups consumed by a household in the previous 24 hours. It measures dietary quality and household food access.

Developed by: FAO
Recall period: 24 hours (yesterday)
Result: Score of 0-12 food groups

Difference Between HDDS and FCS

Aspect HDDS FCS
Recall period 24 hours (yesterday) 7 days
What it measures Variety/diversity only Diversity + frequency + nutritional weights
Food groups 12 groups 8 groups
Scoring Simple count (0-12) Weighted sum (0-112)
Ease Very simple, quick More complex
Best for Quick snapshot, diversity focus Comprehensive food security, WFP standard

The 12 HDDS Food Groups

# Food Group Examples
1 Cereals Maize, rice, wheat, sorghum, millet, bread, pasta
2 Roots & tubers Cassava, sweet potatoes, yams, Irish potatoes
3 Vegetables Tomatoes, onions, spinach, kale, cabbage, carrots
4 Fruits Bananas, mangoes, oranges, papaya, watermelon
5 Meat Beef, goat, chicken, pork, lamb
6 Eggs Chicken eggs, duck eggs

11 Sugar & honey Sugar, honey, jam
12 Miscellaneous Spices, tea, coffee, salt, condiments

How to Calculate HDDS

ℹ️ Note

HDDS Calculation Steps:
  1. Ask: "Yesterday (24 hours), did anyone in your household eat [food from each group]?" YES/NO
  2. Record: 1 if YES, 0 if NO for each food group
  3. Sum: Add up all the 1s = HDDS (0-12)
  4. Interpret: Higher score = better dietary diversity

HDDS Interpretation Guidelines

HDDS Score Category Interpretation
0-3 Very Low Diversity Severe dietary inadequacy, high food insecurity
4-5 Low Diversity Poor diet quality, food insecure
6-7 Medium Diversity Moderate diet quality, vulnerable
8-12 High Diversity Good diet quality, better food security

ℹ️ Note

Important Note on HDDS Thresholds:

Unlike FCS, there is NO universal threshold for HDDS. Some programs use ≥6 as "adequate" but this varies by context. HDDS is best used to:

  • Compare groups (treatment vs control, baseline vs endline)
  • Track trends over time
  • Show relative dietary diversity

For classification into poor/borderline/acceptable, use FCS instead.

  • ✓ You want to track changes in variety consumed

  • ✓ You need WFP-standard food security classification (Poor/Borderline/Acceptable)
  • ✓ Measuring overall household food security (not just nutrition)

  • ✓ You want frequency AND diversity information

  • ✓ Comprehensive food security and nutrition assessment
  • ✓ You have time and want complementary measures

🪜 Understanding Coping Strategies

ℹ️ Note

WHAT ARE COPING STRATEGIES?

Coping strategies are the behaviors and actions households adopt to manage food shortages and maintain their consumption in the face of shocks or stresses.

They range from minor dietary adjustments to irreversible livelihood destruction.

The Coping Ladder: Three Severity Levels

Households follow a predictable progression as food insecurity worsens. Understanding this ladder is critical for early warning and appropriate response.

LEVEL 1: STRESS STRATEGIES (Reversible)

Timing: First response to mild food shortage

Impact: Minimal long-term harm, easily reversible

Examples:

  • Reduce number of meals (3 → 2 per day)
  • Rely on less preferred, cheaper foods
  • Borrow food or money from relatives/friends
  • Purchase food on credit
  • Limit portion sizes at meals
  • Reduce adult consumption so children can eat

Signal: Household struggling but not yet in danger

LEVEL 2: CRISIS STRATEGIES (Threatening)

Timing: When stress strategies are insufficient

Impact: Threatens future productive capacity, difficult to reverse

Examples:

  • Sell productive assets (chickens, goats, plow)
  • Withdraw children from school
  • Reduce essential non-food expenses (health, education)
  • Send household members to eat elsewhere
  • Harvest immature crops
  • Sell more animals than usual
  • Consume seed stock

Signal: Household sacrificing future for present survival

LEVEL 3: EMERGENCY STRATEGIES (Irreversible)

Timing: Desperate last resort

Impact: Destroys livelihood, creates long-term poverty, irreversible

Examples:

  • Sell land or house
  • Sell last female breeding animals
  • Beg for food/money
  • Entire household migration
  • Sell last productive asset (only ox, only fishing net)
  • Engage in illegal activities for income
  • Child marriage for bride price

Signal: Household in catastrophic crisis, livelihood destroyed

Why the Ladder Matters

ℹ️ Note

🔑 EARLY WARNING PRINCIPLE:

The shift from STRESS to CRISIS coping is your WARNING SIGNAL that food insecurity is escalating rapidly. This is when you must act!

STRESS coping: Monitor, prepare, consider early action
CRISIS coping: Intervene NOW before emergency
EMERGENCY coping: Too late, damage already done

📘 Real Example: Karamoja Early Warning Success (2019)

Context: Drought developing in northeastern Uganda

Month 1 (May): Monitoring shows 45% of households reducing meals from 3 to 2 per day (STRESS coping)

  • Action: Strengthen monitoring, pre-position supplies

Month 2 (June): 35% now selling chickens/goats, 28% withdrawing children from school (CRISIS coping)

  • Action Taken: Emergency response activated
  • Cash transfers to 85,000 households
  • School feeding program expanded
  • Veterinary support to protect remaining livestock
  • Cost: $8 million

Result:

  • Crisis prevented from reaching EMERGENCY level
  • Only 8% sold last breeding animals (vs 40% in 2011 when no early action)
  • 90% of children remained in school
  • Recovery in 8 months (vs 3+ years in 2011)
  • Savings: $8M early action vs estimated $25M+ if reached emergency

Lesson: The shift from stress to crisis coping was the trigger for action, preventing catastrophe.

Context Matters: Adapting the Ladder

ℹ️ Note

⚠️ IMPORTANT:

The specific strategies in each level vary by context. A strategy that is "stress" in one setting may be "crisis" in another.

Example: Selling a goat

  • Pastoralist household with 50 goats: STRESS (minor asset sale)
  • Farming household with 3 goats: CRISIS (significant productive asset)
  • Urban household with 1 goat: EMERGENCY (last asset)

Always adapt coping categories to local context through key informant consultation!

📊 Coping Strategy Index (CSI)

ℹ️ Note

WHAT IS CSI?

The Coping Strategy Index (CSI) is a composite indicator that quantifies household coping behavior by asking about frequency of specific coping strategies over the past 7 days.

Developed by: CARE & WFP
Variants: Reduced CSI (rCSI - 5 questions), Full CSI (10-15 questions)
Output: Numerical score; higher = more severe coping

How CSI Works

For each coping strategy, you ask two questions:

  1. Frequency: "In the past 7 days, how many days did your household [coping behavior]?" (0-7 days)
  2. Severity Weight: Each strategy has a pre-assigned severity weight based on its impact

CSI Calculation: Sum of (Frequency × Severity Weight) for all strategies

Standard Reduced CSI (rCSI) - 5 Questions

The Reduced CSI uses 5 universal coping strategies found across most contexts:

Coping Strategy Severity Weight Category
Rely on less preferred, less expensive foods 1 Stress
Borrow food or rely on help from friends/relatives 2 Stress
Limit portion sizes at meal times 1 Stress
Restrict consumption by adults so children can eat 3 Stress
Reduce number of meals eaten per day 1 Stress

ℹ️ Note

Why rCSI Uses Only Stress Strategies:

The 5-question rCSI focuses on DIETARY coping (food-related behaviors) that are universal and comparable across contexts. Crisis/emergency coping varies too much by context for standardization.

For crisis/emergency coping, use expanded CSI or livelihood coping module.

rCSI Calculation Example

📘 Example Household from Mwanza, Tanzania

Question: "In the past 7 days, how many days did your household..."

Strategy Days (0-7) Weight Score
Rely on less preferred foods 6 1 6
Borrow food from friends/relatives 2 2 4
Limit portion sizes 5 1 5
Adults eat less so children can eat 4 3 12
Reduce number of meals per day 3 1 3
TOTAL rCSI 30

Interpretation: rCSI of 30 indicates moderate food insecurity with frequent dietary coping. This household is stressed but managing. Monitor for any shift to crisis coping.

rCSI Thresholds (General Guidelines)

rCSI Range Classification Interpretation
0-3 No/Low Coping Food secure, minimal stress
4-18 Medium Coping Moderate food insecurity
19+ High Coping Severe food insecurity

ℹ️ Note

⚠️ CRITICAL: Context-Specific Thresholds

The thresholds above are GENERAL. You MUST establish context-specific thresholds based on:

  • Historical baseline data for the area
  • Seasonal patterns (lean vs harvest season)
  • Livelihood zone characteristics

Example: Pastoralist areas may have higher "normal" rCSI (15-20) than agricultural areas (5-10).

ℹ️ Note

CSI Early Warning Approach:
  1. Establish baseline: Measure rCSI during "normal" period
  2. Set thresholds: Define what increase signals concern (e.g., +30% = warning, +50% = alert)
  3. Monitor monthly: Track rCSI trends
  4. Trigger action: When thresholds crossed, activate early response

🍽️ Household Hunger Scale (HHS)

ℹ️ Note

WHAT IS HHS?

The Household Hunger Scale (HHS) is a simple, rapid tool measuring the direct experience of household hunger through 6 questions covering 3 behavioral domains.

Developed by: FANTA (Food and Nutrition Technical Assistance)
Questions: 6 (occurrence + frequency for 3 behaviors)
Recall period: Past 4 weeks (30 days)
Output: Score 0-6, classified into Little/None, Moderate, or Severe hunger

Why HHS Was Developed

HHS was created to be:

  • SIMPLE: Just 6 questions, easy to administer and score
  • UNIVERSAL: Works across diverse cultural and economic contexts
  • DIRECT: Asks about hunger experience, not proxies
  • RAPID: Takes 2-3 minutes per household
  • COMPARABLE: Validated in 24 countries for cross-context comparison

The 6 HHS Questions

HHS asks about THREE hunger-related behaviors, with TWO questions each (occurrence + frequency):

ℹ️ Note

BEHAVIOR 1: No food to eat of any kind

Q1a. In the past 4 weeks, was there ever no food to eat of any kind in your house because of lack of resources to get food?

  • 0 = No (skip to Q2a)
  • 1 = Yes (continue to Q1b)

Q1b. How often did this happen in the past 4 weeks?

  • 1 = Rarely (1-2 times)
  • 2 = Sometimes (3-10 times)
  • 3 = Often (more than 10 times)

ℹ️ Note

BEHAVIOR 2: Going to sleep hungry

Q2a. In the past 4 weeks, did you or any household member go to sleep at night hungry because there was not enough food?

  • 0 = No (skip to Q3a)
  • 1 = Yes (continue to Q2b)

Q2b. How often did this happen in the past 4 weeks?

  • 1 = Rarely (1-2 times)
  • 2 = Sometimes (3-10 times)
  • 3 = Often (more than 10 times)

ℹ️ Note

BEHAVIOR 3: Going a whole day without eating

Q3a. In the past 4 weeks, did you or any household member go a whole day and night without eating anything at all because there was not enough food?

  • 0 = No (end survey)
  • 1 = Yes (continue to Q3b)

Q3b. How often did this happen in the past 4 weeks?

  • 1 = Rarely (1-2 times)
  • 2 = Sometimes (3-10 times)
  • 3 = Often (more than 10 times)

HHS Scoring

ℹ️ Note

Scoring Rules:
  1. If answer to occurrence question (a) = NO (0), score for that behavior = 0
  2. If answer to occurrence question (a) = YES (1):
    • Rarely (1-2 times) = 1 point
    • Sometimes (3-10 times) = 2 points
    • Often (>10 times) = 3 points
  3. Sum scores for all 3 behaviors = HHS (0-6)

HHS Classification

HHS Score Category Interpretation
0-1 Little to No Hunger Household experiencing little to no hunger
2-3 Moderate Hunger Household experiencing moderate hunger
4-6 Severe Hunger Household experiencing severe hunger

HHS Calculation Examples

📘 Example 1: Moderate Hunger Household

Question Response Score
Q1a. No food in house? Yes -
Q1b. How often? Sometimes (3-10 times) 2
Q2a. Go to sleep hungry? Yes -
Q2b. How often? Rarely (1-2 times) 1
Q3a. Whole day without eating? No 0
TOTAL HHS 3 (Moderate Hunger)

📘 Example 2: Severe Hunger Household

Question Response Score
Q1a. No food in house? Yes -
Q1b. How often? Often (>10 times) 3
Q2a. Go to sleep hungry? Yes -
Q2b. How often? Sometimes (3-10 times) 2
Q3a. Whole day without eating? Yes -
Q3b. How often? Rarely (1-2 times) 1
TOTAL HHS 6 (Severe Hunger)

When to Use HHS

ℹ️ Note

HHS is BEST for:
  • ✓ Rapid assessments when time is limited
  • ✓ Emergency contexts where simple, direct hunger measure needed
  • ✓ Comparing hunger prevalence across different regions
  • ✓ Tracking changes in hunger over time
  • ✓ Contexts where culturally sensitive to ask direct hunger questions
  • ✓ Validating other food security indicators
HHS Limitations:
  • ✗ Doesn't measure food quality/dietary diversity
  • ✗ Doesn't capture livelihood coping strategies
  • ✗ May miss households coping successfully but unsustainably
  • ✗ 4-week recall may not capture seasonal variations

🌍 Food Insecurity Experience Scale (FIES)

ℹ️ Note

WHAT IS FIES?

The Food Insecurity Experience Scale (FIES) is an 8-question survey measuring household food insecurity based on direct experiences and behaviors, ranging from anxiety about food to severe hunger.

Developed by: FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization)
Questions: 8 yes/no questions
Recall period: Past 12 months
Used for: SDG 2.1.2 monitoring (global food insecurity prevalence)

FIES vs HHS: Key Differences

Aspect HHS FIES
Questions 6 (3 behaviors × 2) 8 (yes/no)
Recall period 4 weeks (30 days) 12 months
Focus Direct hunger experience Food insecurity experience (anxiety → hunger)
Severity levels 3 (Little/Moderate/Severe) 4 (Food Secure/Mild/Moderate/Severe)
Best for Rapid hunger assessment Annual monitoring, SDG reporting, global comparison

The 8 FIES Questions

FIES questions follow a severity progression from WORRY to STARVATION:

ℹ️ Note

Instructions:

"I am going to read you several statements that people have made about their food situation. For each statement, please tell me if the statement was TRUE for you or anyone in your household in the LAST 12 MONTHS (that is, at any time during the past year)."

# Question Severity
1 You were worried you would not have enough food to eat because of a lack of money or other resources MILD ↓
2 You were unable to eat healthy and nutritious food because of a lack of money or other resources MILD ↓
3 You ate only a few kinds of foods because of a lack of money or other resources MILD ↓
4 You had to skip a meal because there was not enough money or other resources to get food MODERATE ↓
5 You ate less than you thought you should because of a lack of money or other resources MODERATE ↓
6 Your household ran out of food because of a lack of money or other resources MODERATE ↓
7 You were hungry but did not eat because there was not enough money or other resources for food SEVERE ↓
8 You went without eating for a whole day because of a lack of money or other resources SEVERE ↓

FIES Scoring

ℹ️ Note

Simple Raw Score Method:
  1. For each question, mark 1 if YES, 0 if NO
  2. Sum all YES responses = Raw Score (0-8)
  3. Classify based on raw score thresholds

FIES Classification (Raw Score)

Raw Score Classification Description
0 Food Secure No food insecurity experienced
1-3 Mild Food Insecurity Worry about food, compromised diet quality
4-6 Moderate Food Insecurity Reduced food intake, skipped meals
7-8 Severe Food Insecurity Severe hunger, whole days without food

ℹ️ Note

⚠️ IMPORTANT: Rasch Model vs Raw Score

For official SDG 2.1.2 reporting to FAO, a complex statistical model (Rasch model) is used to calibrate scores. For program-level use, the simple raw score method above is sufficient.

Question Response Score

5. Ate less than you should YES 1
6. Household ran out of food NO 0
7. Hungry but did not eat NO 0
8. Went whole day without eating NO 0

Interpretation: This household experienced moderate food insecurity over the past year, including worry, poor diet quality, and reduced food intake, but not severe hunger.

When to Use FIES

ℹ️ Note

FIES is BEST for:
  • ✓ Annual food insecurity monitoring (SDG 2.1.2)
  • ✓ Global/regional comparisons (standardized worldwide)
  • ✓ Capturing full spectrum from worry to starvation
  • ✓ Understanding household experience of food insecurity
  • ✓ Policy evaluation at national level
FIES Limitations:
  • ✗ 12-month recall may have memory bias
  • ✗ Not suitable for rapid emergency assessments (use HHS instead)
  • ✗ Doesn't measure coping strategies or causes
  • ✗ Doesn't provide information on dietary quality specifics

Why Nutrition Indicators Matter

Food security and nutrition are intimately connected but NOT the same:

ℹ️ Note

CRITICAL DISTINCTION:

Food Security: Availability, access, and utilization of food
Nutrition Security: Adequate nutritional status resulting from food intake, care practices, health, and sanitation

You can have:

  • ✓ Food security WITHOUT nutrition security (adequate food quantity but poor quality/utilization)
  • ✓ Malnutrition DESPITE food availability (poor feeding practices, disease, unsafe water)

📘 Real Example: Uganda Paradox (2018)

Situation: Bushenyi District, southwestern Uganda

Food Security Assessment:

  • FCS: 78% acceptable consumption
  • Food availability: Harvest season, markets well-stocked
  • Assessment conclusion: Food secure

Nutrition Survey Reveals:

  • Stunting (chronic malnutrition): 32% of children under 5
  • Anemia: 51% of children 6-59 months
  • Exclusive breastfeeding (0-6 months): Only 41%

Root Causes Found:

  • Limited dietary diversity (heavy reliance on matoke/plantains)
  • Poor infant feeding practices (early introduction of water/porridge)
  • High disease burden (malaria, diarrhea reducing nutrient absorption)
  • Poor water/sanitation (41% open defecation)

Lesson: Food security indicators showed "acceptable" situation, but nutrition indicators revealed severe hidden malnutrition. Both assessments are essential!

📏 Anthropometric Indicators

ℹ️ Note

WHAT IS ANTHROPOMETRY?

Anthropometry is the measurement of the human body. In nutrition, we measure body size (weight, height, arm circumference) to assess nutritional status, particularly in children under 5 years.

Why children under 5? Most vulnerable to malnutrition, easier to measure, reflects household food security

The Three Core Anthropometric Indicators

We combine weight, height, and age to create three key indicators:

Core Anthropometric Indices

1. HEIGHT-FOR-AGE (Stunting)

What: Child's height compared to expected height for their age

Measures: CHRONIC malnutrition (long-term)

Threshold: < -2 SD = Stunted

Meaning: Past deprivation, irreversible

2. WEIGHT-FOR-HEIGHT (Wasting)

What: Child's weight compared to expected weight for their height

Measures: ACUTE malnutrition (current)

Threshold: < -2 SD = Wasted (MAM), < -3 SD = Severely wasted (SAM)

Meaning: Recent/current crisis, reversible

3. WEIGHT-FOR-AGE (Underweight)

What: Child's weight compared to expected weight for their age

Measures: OVERALL malnutrition (composite)

Threshold: < -2 SD = Underweight

Meaning: Can reflect acute or chronic or both

1. Stunting (Height-for-Age) - Chronic Malnutrition

ℹ️ Note

STUNTING DEFINITION:

Child is TOO SHORT for their age due to prolonged inadequate nutrition, repeated infections, and poor care practices during the first 1,000 days (pregnancy to 2 years).

Classification: Height-for-age Z-score < -2 SD from WHO reference median

Z-Score Range Classification Prevalence Threshold Public Health Significance
≥ -2 SD Normal - -
< -2 SD Stunted < 20% Low
20-29% Medium
30-39% High
≥ 40% Very High
< -3 SD Severely Stunted - Part of overall stunting

Key Facts About Stunting:

  • Window of opportunity: First 1,000 days (conception to 2 years) - mostly IRREVERSIBLE after age 2
  • Consequences: Reduced cognitive development, poor school performance, lower economic productivity, intergenerational cycle
  • Causes: Chronic food insecurity, poor maternal nutrition, inadequate infant feeding, repeated infections, poor WASH
  • EAC Regional Data (2023): Burundi 55%, Tanzania 31%, Uganda 29%, Kenya 26%, Rwanda 33%

2. Wasting (Weight-for-Height) - Acute Malnutrition

ℹ️ Note

WASTING DEFINITION:

Child is TOO THIN for their height due to recent rapid weight loss or failure to gain weight, typically from acute food shortage or severe illness.

Classifications:

  • GAM (Global Acute Malnutrition): WHZ < -2 SD OR bilateral pitting edema
  • MAM (Moderate Acute Malnutrition): -3 SD ≤ WHZ < -2 SD, no edema
  • SAM (Severe Acute Malnutrition): WHZ < -3 SD OR bilateral pitting edema
GAM Prevalence Public Health Significance IPC Acute Malnutrition Phase
< 5% Acceptable Phase 1 (None/Minimal)
5-9.9% Poor Phase 2 (Alert)
10-14.9% Serious Phase 3 (Serious)
≥ 15% Critical Phase 4 (Critical)
≥ 30% Extremely Critical Phase 5 (Catastrophic)

Key Facts About Wasting:

  • Timing: Reflects CURRENT/RECENT malnutrition (past 3-6 months)
  • Seasonality: Often peaks during lean season (pre-harvest)
  • Reversibility: Can be treated with therapeutic feeding (REVERSIBLE)
  • Mortality risk: SAM children are 9x more likely to die than well-nourished children
  • Emergency threshold: GAM ≥ 15% = critical, requires emergency response

3. Underweight (Weight-for-Age) - Composite Indicator

ℹ️ Note

UNDERWEIGHT DEFINITION:

Child is TOO LIGHT for their age. Reflects both acute and chronic malnutrition but cannot distinguish between them.

Classification: Weight-for-age Z-score < -2 SD

Z-Score Range Classification
≥ -2 SD Normal weight
-3 SD ≤ WAZ < -2 SD Moderately Underweight
< -3 SD Severely Underweight

Why Underweight is Less Used Now:

  • Cannot distinguish acute from chronic malnutrition
  • A child can be stunted AND wasted, or only stunted, or only wasted - underweight doesn't tell you which
  • Modern practice: Report stunting AND wasting separately rather than just underweight
  • Still used: SDG indicator 2.2.1 (prevalence of stunting and wasting separately), some national surveys

Interpreting Anthropometric Patterns

📊 What Different Patterns Tell You

Pattern 1: High Stunting, Low Wasting

  • Example: Stunting 35%, Wasting 4%
  • Meaning: Long-term chronic food insecurity and poverty, but no current acute crisis
  • Response: Long-term development programs (agriculture, livelihoods, WASH, nutrition-sensitive interventions)

Pattern 2: Low Stunting, High Wasting

  • Example: Stunting 12%, Wasting 14%
  • Meaning: Acute crisis (drought, flood, displacement) in previously stable area
  • Response: Emergency nutrition intervention (SAM/MAM treatment, supplementary feeding)

Pattern 3: High Stunting, High Wasting

  • Example: Stunting 40%, Wasting 16%
  • Meaning: Chronic poverty + acute crisis = catastrophic situation
  • Response: Emergency + long-term integrated response

Pattern 4: Low Stunting, Low Wasting

  • Example: Stunting 15%, Wasting 3%
  • Meaning: Good nutrition situation (but still monitor for pockets of vulnerability)
  • Response: Maintain, focus on prevention and specific high-risk groups

🎯 MUAC Measurement

ℹ️ Note

WHAT IS MUAC?

Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC) is the measurement of the circumference of the left upper arm at the mid-point between the shoulder and elbow.

Why MUAC? Simple, fast, requires minimal training, strong predictor of mortality risk, used for SAM screening and admission to treatment programs

MUAC vs Weight-for-Height: Key Differences

Aspect MUAC Weight-for-Height
Equipment MUAC tape (cheap, ~$0.50) Scale + height board (expensive, ~$200+)
Training needed Minimal (1-2 hours) Significant (2-3 days)
Time per child 30 seconds 3-5 minutes
Age range 6-59 months 6-59 months (or 65-110 cm height)
Best for Screening, community mobilization, admission to treatment Nutrition surveys, precise prevalence estimates
Mortality prediction Better predictor Good predictor
Mother can do it YES (with color-coded tapes) NO (requires trained staff)

MUAC Classification Thresholds

MUAC Color Zones (6-59 months)

RED Zone

< 115 mm

Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM)

Action: Immediate referral to therapeutic feeding program

YELLOW Zone

115 to < 125 mm

Moderate Acute Malnutrition (MAM)

Action: Supplementary feeding program

GREEN Zone

≥ 125 mm

Well-nourished

Action: Continue good feeding practices

ℹ️ Note

⚠️ BILATERAL PITTING EDEMA:

Regardless of MUAC, a child with bilateral pitting edema (swelling in both feet that leaves an impression when pressed) is automatically classified as SAM and requires immediate treatment.

How to check: Press thumb firmly on both feet for 3 seconds. If impression remains for ≥3 seconds on both feet = edema = SAM

How to Measure MUAC Correctly

ℹ️ Note

MUAC Measurement Steps:
  1. Verify age: Child must be 6-59 months (or if no age, height 65-110 cm)
  2. Position child: Standing or sitting, relaxed, left arm hanging loose
  3. Find mid-point:
    • Locate tip of shoulder (acromion process)
    • Bend elbow 90°, locate tip of elbow (olecranon process)
    • Measure distance between these two points
    • Mark the mid-point with pen/finger
  4. Straighten arm: Arm hangs straight down, relaxed
  5. Wrap MUAC tape:
    • Wrap around arm at mid-point mark
    • Tape should be snug but not tight (not compressing flesh)
    • Tape perpendicular to arm (not slanted)
  6. Read measurement: Read at the window/arrow to nearest 1 mm
  7. Classify: Check color zone (Red/Yellow/Green) or numeric value
  8. Check for edema: Always check both feet for bilateral pitting edema

ℹ️ Note

Common MUAC Measurement Errors:
  • ✗ Using right arm instead of left (always use LEFT arm)
  • ✗ Tape too tight (compressing muscle)
  • ✗ Tape too loose (giving false high reading)
  • ✗ Wrong mid-point (must be exact halfway between shoulder and elbow)
  • ✗ Arm not relaxed (child crying, arm tense)
  • ✗ Tape slanted instead of perpendicular
  • ✗ Measuring over thick clothing

MUAC in Practice: Screening vs Surveys

Purpose MUAC Use Setting
Community Screening Identify SAM/MAM children for treatment admission Health posts, mobile clinics, community health workers, mass screening campaigns
Nutrition Surveys Estimate prevalence of acute malnutrition in population SMART surveys, rapid nutrition assessments
Discharge Criteria Determine when child is recovered from SAM/MAM OTP (Outpatient Therapeutic Program), SFP (Supplementary Feeding Program)
Mother MUAC Mothers measure own children, self-refer to clinic if red/yellow Community-based management of acute malnutrition (CMAM)

MUAC vs WHZ: Which Children Identified?

ℹ️ Note

IMPORTANT: MUAC and WHZ Don't Identify the Same Children

Studies show only 40-60% overlap between children identified as SAM by MUAC vs WHZ.

MUAC identifies: Younger, shorter, more at risk of death

WHZ identifies: Older, taller children who are thin

WHO Recommendation (2013): Use BOTH criteria. A child meets SAM if EITHER MUAC < 115 mm OR WHZ < -3 OR bilateral edema.

📘 Case Example: MUAC Screening Success, Turkana Kenya (2021)

Context: Remote pastoralist area, drought developing

Challenge: No nutrition survey for 18 months (insecurity), no data on malnutrition levels

Solution: Train 120 community health volunteers to do MUAC screening

  • Training: 3 hours on MUAC measurement + edema checking
  • Equipment: Color-coded MUAC tapes ($0.50 each)
  • Strategy: House-to-house screening, all children 6-59 months

Results in 2 weeks:

  • Screened: 8,450 children
  • SAM identified (MUAC < 115): 412 children (4.9%)
  • MAM identified (MUAC 115-124): 1,014 children (12%)
  • Cost per child screened: $0.32

Action Taken:

  • All 412 SAM children admitted to OTP (outpatient therapeutic program)
  • All 1,014 MAM children enrolled in SFP (supplementary feeding program)
  • Total GAM rate (16.9%) triggered scale-up of emergency nutrition response

Outcome: MUAC screening provided rapid data for emergency response decision, identified and referred vulnerable children, all at minimal cost. Full SMART survey confirmed results 4 weeks later.

👶 Infant and Young Child Feeding (IYCF) Indicators

ℹ️ Note

WHY IYCF MATTERS:

The first 1,000 days (pregnancy to age 2) are the most critical for nutrition. Poor feeding practices during this window lead to irreversible stunting, illness, and developmental delays.

IYCF indicators measure: Breastfeeding practices and complementary feeding quality

The 8 Core IYCF Indicators (WHO 2021)

WHO defines 8 core indicators to assess infant and young child feeding practices:

Indicator Age Group What It Measures
1. Early initiation of breastfeeding 0-23 months % put to breast within 1 hour of birth
2. Exclusive breastfeeding < 6 months 0-5 months % receiving only breastmilk (no water/other foods)
3. Continued breastfeeding at 1 year 12-15 months % still breastfeeding
4. Continued breastfeeding at 2 years 20-23 months % still breastfeeding
5. Introduction of solid/semi-solid foods 6-8 months % receiving complementary foods
6. Minimum dietary diversity (MDD) 6-23 months % consuming ≥5 of 8 food groups
7. Minimum meal frequency (MMF) 6-23 months % fed minimum # times (varies by breastfeeding status)
8. Minimum acceptable diet (MAD) 6-23 months % meeting both MDD AND MMF

Key IYCF Indicators Explained

1. Exclusive Breastfeeding (EBF) - Birth to 6 Months

ℹ️ Note

DEFINITION:

Infant receives ONLY breastmilk (including expressed milk) from birth to 6 months. NO water, NO other liquids, NO other foods.

Exceptions allowed: Oral rehydration solution (ORS), vitamins, minerals, medicines

Why Exclusive Breastfeeding Matters:

  • Breastmilk contains all nutrients and water needed for first 6 months
  • Protects against diarrhea, respiratory infections (immunological protection)
  • Reduces risk of stunting, obesity, diabetes later in life
  • Introducing water/other foods too early increases infection risk and reduces breastmilk intake
EBF Rate Assessment
≥ 70% Excellent
50-69% Good
30-49% Fair
< 30% Poor

EAC Regional Data (2020-2023):

  • Rwanda: 87% (Excellent - highest in Africa!)
  • Burundi: 82% (Excellent)
  • Uganda: 65% (Good)
  • Tanzania: 59% (Good)
  • Kenya: 61% (Good)

2. Minimum Dietary Diversity (MDD) - 6-23 Months

ℹ️ Note

DEFINITION:

Child 6-23 months consumed foods/beverages from at least 5 out of 8 food groups in the previous 24 hours.

The 8 Food Groups for MDD:

  1. Breastmilk
  2. Grains, roots, tubers (maize, rice, cassava, sweet potato, bread)
  3. Pulses (legumes), nuts, seeds (beans, lentils, peas, groundnuts)
  4. Dairy products (milk, yogurt, cheese) - excluding breastmilk
  5. Flesh foods (meat, fish, poultry, liver/organ meats)
  6. Eggs
  7. Vitamin A-rich fruits and vegetables (mango, papaya, carrots, pumpkin, dark green leafy vegetables)
  8. Other fruits and vegetables (banana, apple, tomato, onion, etc.)

Scoring: Consumed ≥5 groups = Meets MDD (Yes = 1, No = 0)

📘 MDD Calculation Example

Child Age: 12 months
Foods consumed in past 24 hours:

  1. ✓ Breastmilk (multiple times) = Group 1 ✓
  2. ✓ Ugali (maize porridge) = Group 2 ✓
  3. ✓ Beans = Group 3 ✓
  4. ✗ No dairy
  5. ✗ No flesh foods
  6. ✗ No eggs
  7. ✓ Sukuma wiki (dark green leafy vegetable) = Group 7 ✓
  8. ✓ Banana = Group 8 ✓

Result: 5 food groups consumed → MEETS MDD ✓

3. Minimum Meal Frequency (MMF) - 6-23 Months

ℹ️ Note

DEFINITION:

Child fed the minimum number of times appropriate for their age and breastfeeding status in the previous 24 hours.

Age Breastfed Non-Breastfed
6-8 months ≥ 2 times/day ≥ 4 times/day
9-23 months ≥ 3 times/day ≥ 4 times/day

Note: Non-breastfed children require more frequent feeding because they don't have the on-demand nutrition from breastmilk.

4. Minimum Acceptable Diet (MAD) - 6-23 Months

ℹ️ Note

DEFINITION:

Composite indicator. Child receives BOTH:

  • Minimum Dietary Diversity (MDD) ✓ AND
  • Minimum Meal Frequency (MMF) ✓

PLUS breastfed children must be breastfeeding, non-breastfed must receive ≥2 milk feeds

Why MAD is Important:

  • Most comprehensive single indicator of feeding quality
  • Strong predictor of nutritional status
  • Used for program targeting and impact evaluation
  • SDG Indicator 2.2.2a (% children 6-23 months with MAD)

ℹ️ Note

⚠️ Alarming EAC MAD Rates:

Despite high EBF rates, complementary feeding quality is poor:

  • Rwanda: 22% MAD
  • Kenya: 24% MAD
  • Tanzania: 27% MAD
  • Uganda: 16% MAD
  • Burundi: 12% MAD

This means 75-88% of children 6-23 months are NOT receiving adequate diet! This is a major driver of stunting in the region.

Assessing IYCF in Surveys

ℹ️ Note

IYCF Data Collection Method:
  1. 24-hour dietary recall: Ask mother what child consumed in previous 24 hours
  2. Probe systematically: Go through all food groups, ask about snacks, meals away from home
  3. Record all foods/beverages: List everything consumed
  4. Classify foods: Code each food into one of the 8 food groups
  5. Calculate indicators: MDD, MMF, MAD based on data collected

📊 Survey Design & Sampling Methods

ℹ️ Note

WHY SAMPLING MATTERS:

You cannot measure every household. You must select a representative SAMPLE that allows you to make valid conclusions about the entire POPULATION.

Bad sampling = Biased results = Wrong decisions = Failed interventions

Types of Food Security & Nutrition Surveys

Survey Type Purpose Typical Sample Duration
SMART Survey Standardized nutrition + mortality assessment in emergencies 300-900 HH 2-3 weeks
Comprehensive Food Security Assessment In-depth FS analysis (FCS, CSI, HHS, livelihoods) 1,200-2,400 HH 4-6 weeks
Rapid Assessment Quick snapshot for emergency decision (HHS, FCS) 200-400 HH 3-7 days
Demographic & Health Survey (DHS) National representative survey (health, nutrition, population) 8,000-15,000 HH 6-12 months
Post-Distribution Monitoring Verify aid delivery and measure immediate outcomes 100-300 HH 1-2 weeks

Sampling Methods

1. Simple Random Sampling

ℹ️ Note

HOW IT WORKS:

Every household has equal probability of selection. Like lottery - put all household names in a hat, randomly draw sample.

Pros: Unbiased, statistically valid
Cons: Requires complete household list (sampling frame), may be geographically scattered (high cost)

Best for: Small, defined populations (e.g., camp, single village)

2. Systematic Random Sampling

ℹ️ Note

HOW IT WORKS:

Select every Kth household from a list.

Example: Population 5,000 HH, sample 500 needed → K = 5,000/500 = 10 → Select every 10th household

Process: Start at random number (1-10), then select every 10th: 7, 17, 27, 37...

Pros: Simple, ensures spread
Cons: Requires ordered list, can be biased if list has patterns

Best for: Sampling from registries, camp lists

3. Stratified Random Sampling

ℹ️ Note

HOW IT WORKS:

Divide population into subgroups (strata), then randomly sample from each stratum.

Example Strata: Livelihood zones, geographic regions, urban/rural, wealth groups

Pros: Ensures representation of all subgroups, allows sub-group analysis
Cons: Requires knowledge of stratum proportions, more complex

Best for: Diverse populations where subgroup comparison needed

4. Cluster Sampling (Most Common in FNS Surveys)

ℹ️ Note

HOW IT WORKS:

Two-stage process:

  1. Stage 1: Randomly select clusters (villages/EAs - Enumeration Areas)
  2. Stage 2: Within each selected cluster, randomly select households

Example: 30 clusters × 20 households/cluster = 600 households total

Pros: Cost-effective (teams stay in cluster area), feasible without complete HH list
Cons: Less precise than simple random (design effect), requires larger sample

Best for: Large geographic areas, SMART surveys, national surveys

Sample Size Determination

ℹ️ Note

Factors Affecting Sample Size:
  1. Expected prevalence: The indicator you're measuring (e.g., GAM = 15%)
  2. Desired precision: How accurate you want to be (±3%, ±5%)
  3. Confidence level: Usually 95% (Z = 1.96)
  4. Design effect: Accounts for cluster sampling (typically 1.5-2.0)
  5. Non-response rate: Expected % who won't participate (5-10%)

Standard Sample Sizes for Nutrition Surveys:

Survey Type Children (6-59 mo) Households Clusters
SMART (district level) 300-450 300-450 30 × 10-15 HH
SMART (with mortality) 600-900 600-900 30 × 20-30 HH
Regional comparison 900+ (300 per region) 900+ Varies

Selection of Households in the Field

ℹ️ Note

Common Methods for HH Selection Within Clusters:

Method 1: Random Walk / Spin the Pen

  1. Team arrives at cluster center (village center/chief's house)
  2. Spin pen/bottle on ground to determine random direction
  3. Walk in that direction, counting households
  4. Select every Kth household (e.g., every 3rd) until sample reached
  5. If reach end of village, spin again from center in new direction

Method 2: Segmentation

  1. Divide cluster into segments (usually 4-6 segments)
  2. List all households in each segment
  3. Randomly select proportional number from each segment

Method 3: Household List (if available)

  1. Obtain complete list of households in cluster from village leader
  2. Number all households
  3. Use random number table or ENA software to select households

ℹ️ Note

⚠️ AVOID THESE SAMPLING BIASES:
  • ✗ Only selecting households near road (road bias)
  • ✗ Only visiting when convenient for team (missing working families)
  • ✗ Replacing difficult-to-reach HH with nearby HH (introduces bias)
  • ✗ Asking village leader to "select typical households" (selection bias)
  • ✗ Surveying only households present at first visit (non-response bias)

Always use probability-based random selection!

✅ Data Quality Assurance

ℹ️ Note

WHY DATA QUALITY MATTERS:

"Garbage in, garbage out." Poor quality data leads to wrong conclusions, inappropriate responses, and wasted resources.

Quality data requires: Proper training, standardized procedures, supervision, and verification at every step.

The Data Quality Assurance Framework

Three Stages of Quality Control

BEFORE Survey

Training & Standardization

  • Enumerator training (5-7 days)
  • Standardization tests (pass/fail)
  • Equipment calibration
  • Pre-test questionnaires
  • Clear SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures)
DURING Survey

Field Monitoring

  • Daily data checks
  • Supervisor spot-checks (10% re-measurement)
  • Plausibility checks
  • Review for outliers
  • Immediate feedback to teams
AFTER Survey

Data Cleaning & Analysis

  • Completeness checks
  • Range checks
  • Plausibility analysis (WHO flags)
  • Outlier investigation
  • Data quality report

Standardization Tests for Anthropometry

ℹ️ Note

STANDARDIZATION TEST PURPOSE:

Verify that enumerators can measure weight and height accurately and consistently before deploying to the field.

Standard Protocol:

  1. Bring 10 children of varying ages (6-59 months)
  2. Each enumerator measures each child twice (20 measurements per enumerator)
  3. Expert supervisor also measures each child (gold standard)
  4. Calculate precision and accuracy for each enumerator

Pass Criteria (WHO Standards):

Measurement Precision (TEM) Accuracy vs Expert
Weight < 100g Mean difference < 100g
Height < 7mm Mean difference < 7mm
MUAC < 2mm Mean difference < 2mm

ℹ️ Note

⚠️ Enumerators who FAIL standardization:
  • Cannot participate in survey
  • Require additional training and re-testing
  • If repeatedly fail, cannot be used for anthropometric surveys

No exceptions! Poor measurement = invalid survey results.

Daily Data Quality Checks

ℹ️ Note

Every Evening During Survey:
  1. Completeness check: All questions answered? No missing data?
  2. Range check: Values within plausible ranges?
    • Weight: 3-30 kg
    • Height: 45-120 cm
    • MUAC: 80-200 mm
    • Age: 6-59 months
  3. Logic check: Answers consistent?
    • If child 6-8 months, should not have "ate meat yesterday" without complementary feeding started
    • If breastfeeding = no, should not score for breastmilk food group
  4. Outlier review: Flag extreme values for verification
    • Weight-for-height Z-score < -5 or > +5
    • Height-for-age Z-score < -6 or > +6

WHO Plausibility Criteria for Anthropometric Data

After data collection, run plausibility checks using ENA for SMART software:

Criteria Excellent Good Acceptable Problematic
Overall data quality score 0-4 5-9 10-14 ≥ 15
% children with WHZ flags < 2.5% 2.5-5% 5-7.5% > 7.5%
% children with HAZ flags < 2.5% 2.5-5% 5-7.5% > 7.5%
Standard deviation of WHZ < 1.10 1.10-1.15 1.15-1.20 > 1.20
Digit preference (weight) < 7 7-12 12-20 > 20

ℹ️ Note

INTERPRETING QUALITY SCORES:
  • Excellent (0-4): Data highly reliable, publish with confidence
  • Good (5-9): Data acceptable, minor issues don't affect overall validity
  • Acceptable (10-14): Data usable but with caveats, mention limitations in report
  • Problematic (≥15): Data quality poor, results may not be reliable, investigate errors

Common Data Quality Problems and Solutions

Problem Likely Cause Solution
High WHZ flags (> 7.5%) Measurement errors in weight/height Re-train teams, verify equipment calibration, increase supervision
SD of WHZ > 1.20 Inconsistent measurements between teams Re-standardization, pair weak with strong measurers
Digit preference (weight ending in 0 or 5) Not reading scale precisely Re-train on scale reading, enforce recording to 0.1 kg
Age heaping (many children "24 months") Mothers don't know exact age, enumerators guessing Use event calendar, require birth documentation where possible
Too many missing values Incomplete interviews, refusals Improve community mobilization, revisit households, extend survey if needed

📘 Case: Data Quality Saves Survey - Kisumu, Kenya (2020)

Situation: SMART survey, 450 children measured over 10 days

Day 3 Evening Check: Supervisor notices Team 2 has WHZ SD = 1.45 (very high, should be < 1.20)

Investigation:

  • Re-measure 10 children from Team 2
  • Discover: Height board not positioned correctly against wall
  • Result: All heights measured 2-3 cm too short
  • Impact: Artificially inflated wasting rates

Action Taken:

  • Discard all Day 1-3 data from Team 2
  • Re-train Team 2 on height measurement
  • Re-visit all 45 households to re-measure children
  • Extend survey by 2 days to complete sample

Outcome:

  • Final data quality score: 6 (Good)
  • Without correction, would have reported 18.5% GAM (emergency threshold)
  • Corrected result: 9.2% GAM (poor but not emergency)
  • Daily quality checks prevented launch of unnecessary emergency response (saving $2+ million)

🔗 Integrating Food Security & Nutrition Assessments

ℹ️ Note

WHY INTEGRATE?

Food security and nutrition are interconnected. Assessing only one gives incomplete picture and leads to inappropriate responses.

Integrated assessment = Comprehensive understanding = Appropriate, effective response

What Does Integration Mean?

Integration has TWO meanings:

ℹ️ Note

1. COMBINED DATA COLLECTION (Same survey collects both FS and nutrition data)

Benefits:

  • Cost-effective (one team, one survey instead of two)
  • Reduced survey fatigue on communities
  • Same sampling frame = directly comparable results
  • Enables analysis of FS-nutrition linkages

Typical Combination:

  • Anthropometry (stunting, wasting, MUAC)
  • + FCS (food consumption score)
  • + rCSI (coping strategies)
  • + HHS or FIES (hunger/food insecurity experience)

🏛️ The Four Pillars and Assessment Types

The Four Pillars of Food Security

1. AVAILABILITY - Physical presence of food

Definition: The physical availability of food in sufficient quantities and quality to meet dietary needs.

Influenced by:

  • Food production levels
  • Distribution systems
  • Trade (imports and exports)
  • Stock levels

Indicators in this pillar: 4-9 (Dietary Energy Supply, Macronutrients, Import Dependency, Self-Sufficiency)

2. ACCESS - Ability to obtain food

Definition: An individual's ability to obtain food through production, purchasing power, and resources.

Influenced by:

  • Poverty and inequality
  • Food prices
  • Income levels
  • Employment opportunities
  • Market access

Indicators in this pillar: 1-3 (Severe Food Insecurity, Dietary Energy Consumption, Prevalence of Undernourishment)

3. UTILIZATION - Proper use and absorption of nutrients

Definition: The ability to properly use and absorb food consumed, ensuring adequate nutrient intake.

Influenced by:

  • Water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH)
  • Health status and disease burden
  • Care practices (especially for children)
  • Food safety and preparation
  • Dietary diversity

Indicators in this pillar: 12-21 (Anaemia, Vitamin A, Stunting, Wasting, Underweight, Low Birth Weight, Adolescent Overweight)

4. STABILITY - Maintenance of food security over time

Definition: The resilience of the food system in the face of challenges and disruptions.

Influenced by:

  • Governance and policy frameworks
  • Infrastructure quality
  • Technology and innovation
  • Climate resilience
  • Conflict and security

Indicators in this pillar: 10-11 (Food Price Index, Food Price Volatility)

Types of Assessments for FNS Monitoring

Assessment Type Purpose Timeframe Key Characteristics
Chronic Food Security Monitor long-term structural food insecurity Annual/Multi-year Uses core indicators; measures persistent conditions
Acute Food Security Identify emergency situations requiring urgent response Real-time/Seasonal Uses IPC, CARI; classifies severity of crises
Transitory Food Security Monitor temporary food insecurity from shocks Quarterly/Seasonal Uses HEA, early warning indicators
Comprehensive Nutrition Assess nutritional status and determinants 3-5 years Anthropometric measurements, biomarkers

ℹ️ Note

Important Note: The 23 core indicators primarily focus on chronic food and nutrition security assessment. For acute and transitory situations, alternative rapid methodologies (IPC, CARI, HEA) are used in conjunction with selected core indicators.

80-99% Minor production deficit Some import needs, manageable

⚠️ Important

Countries with High Import Dependency Faced:

Sharper food price increases

Greater vulnerability to global market disruptions

Higher food import bills
Countries spent more on importing the same quantity of food, straining foreign exchange reserves
Increased food insecurity
Higher import costs translated to higher domestic prices, reducing household food access

📊 Introduction to Food Security Stability

Recall from our conceptual framework that stability relates to the resilience of the food system in the face of challenges and disruptions.

ℹ️ Note

💡
Key Question

The stability pillar addresses: "Can people maintain access to adequate food over time?"

A population may have sufficient food availability and access today, but without stability, they remain vulnerable to future food insecurity.

Indicator 10: Food Price Index

Definition and Measurement

The Food Price Index measures changes in international food prices. It is expressed as an index value, typically with a base year set at 100.

Attribute Detail
Unit of Measurement Index
Custodian Agency FAO
Geographical Coverage National, Regional, Global
Timeliness One month lag

Methodology

The Food Price Index is calculated as a weighted average of price indices for various food commodities:

  • Cereals
  • Vegetable oils
  • Dairy products
  • Meat
  • Sugar

Importance for Food Security Monitoring

1. Early Warning System

Rising food prices can signal potential food security crises, particularly for net food-importing countries and poor households who spend a large proportion of their income on food.

2. Policy Response Trigger

Sharp increases in the index can prompt governments to implement safety net programs, subsidies, or strategic grain reserve releases.

3. Trade and Import Planning

Countries can use price trends to make informed decisions about food imports and trade agreements.

⚠️ Important

⚠️
EAC Context

For EAC Partner States, monitoring the Food Price Index is particularly important because:

  • Most EAC countries are net food importers for certain commodities
  • Household food expenditure represents 50-60% of total spending in many areas
  • Price volatility can quickly push vulnerable populations into food insecurity
Indicator 11: Food Price Volatility

Definition and Measurement

Food Price Volatility measures the degree of variation in food prices over time. It captures the unpredictability and instability of food prices rather than just their level.

Measurement Method Description
Standard Deviation Measures absolute variation in prices around the mean
Coefficient of Variation (CV) Standard deviation ÷ mean (expressed as %)
Preferred method for comparisons
Price Variance Square of the standard deviation

Interpretation Guidelines

✅ Key points

✓
CV < 15%: Low volatility - stable market conditions

⚠️ Important

⚠️
CV 15-30%: Moderate volatility - some market instability

⚠️ Caution

⚠️
CV > 30%: High volatility - significant market dysfunction

Impact on Food Security

  • Planning Uncertainty: High volatility makes it difficult for households to plan food purchases and budgets
  • Market Inefficiency: Volatility indicates market dysfunction and poor integration
  • Investment Deterrent: Farmers and traders avoid markets with high price volatility
  • Coping Strategy Disruption: Unpredictable prices make traditional coping strategies less effective

💡 Recommendations for Partner States

  1. Establish price stabilization mechanisms (strategic reserves, buffer stocks)
  2. Improve market information systems
  3. Invest in storage and transport infrastructure
  4. Harmonize trade policies across the region
  5. Strengthen regional market integration

Four out of seven EAC countries have SEVERE public health problems with childhood anemia (≥40% prevalence). This requires urgent, comprehensive interventions.

Causes of Anemia

  • Iron deficiency (most common cause - 50% of cases)
  • Other micronutrient deficiencies (folate, vitamin B12, vitamin A)
  • Infectious diseases (malaria, hookworm, schistosomiasis)

  2. Food fortification (wheat flour, maize flour, salt)

  4. Malaria prevention and treatment (ITNs, IPT, prompt treatment)
  5. Deworming programs (twice yearly for children in endemic areas)
  6. Improved WASH conditions (reduce infections)

📏 Indicator 17: Prevalence of Stunting (Height-for-Age)

Definition

The percentage of children aged 0-59 months who have height-for-age below minus two standard deviations (-2 SD) from the median of the WHO Child Growth Standards.

Classification

  • Normal: ≥ -2 SD
  • Moderate stunting: < -2 SD to ≥ -3 SD
  • Severe stunting: < -3 SD

ℹ️ Note

🎯
Global Targets:
  • WHO Global Target: Reduce stunting by 40% by 2025 (from 2012 baseline)
  • SDG Target 2.2.1: End all forms of malnutrition by 2030

DRC 42-46% 15-18% Stable/Increasing
Kenya 26-28% 8-10% Declining
Rwanda 33-36% 10-12% Declining
South Sudan 31-35% 10-13% Stable
Tanzania 31-34% 10-12% Declining slowly
Uganda 28-31% 9-11% Declining slowly

⚠️ Caution

⚠️
Critical Situation:

Burundi has one of the highest stunting rates in the world (52-56%). Even the best-performing EAC country (Kenya at 26-28%) still has a HIGH prevalence by WHO standards (>20%).

What Stunting Indicates

Stunting reflects chronic undernutrition and indicates:

Root Causes:

  • Inadequate nutrition during the first 1,000 days (pregnancy to age 2)
  • Repeated infections (diarrhea, respiratory infections)
  • Poor infant and young child feeding practices
  • Household food insecurity
  • Poor WASH conditions
  • Low maternal education and nutrition
  • Poverty and social inequality

Long-Term Consequences:

  • Impaired cognitive development (8-10 point IQ deficit)
  • Reduced educational attainment (1 year less schooling on average)
  • Lower economic productivity in adulthood (20-30% less income)
  • Increased risk of chronic diseases (diabetes, cardiovascular disease)
  • Intergenerational transmission (stunted mothers → low birth weight babies)

⚠️ Important

⏰
Critical Intervention Window

The first 1,000 days (from pregnancy to 24 months of age) is the critical period for preventing stunting. After age 2, stunting is largely irreversible.

Recommended Interventions

💡 Evidence-Based Interventions for Stunting Prevention

  1. Maternal nutrition: Iron-folic acid supplementation, balanced energy-protein supplementation for undernourished pregnant women
  2. Early initiation of breastfeeding: Within 1 hour of birth
  3. Exclusive breastfeeding: 0-6 months
  4. Continued breastfeeding: Up to 24 months with complementary foods
  5. Age-appropriate complementary feeding: Nutrient-dense foods starting at 6 months
  6. Micronutrient supplementation: Vitamin A, iron, zinc, multiple micronutrient powders
  7. WASH interventions: Safe water, improved sanitation, handwashing with soap
  8. Health care: Complete immunization, growth monitoring, prompt treatment of infections
  9. Social protection: Cash transfers, food assistance for vulnerable households

What Wasting Indicates

Wasting indicates acute malnutrition resulting from:

Causes:

  • Recent food shortage or famine
  • Acute illness (severe diarrhea, measles, malaria)
  • Inadequate feeding during illness
  • Seasonal food insecurity
  • Displacement and emergencies

Consequences:

  • Increased mortality risk: 9 times higher for severe wasting, 3 times higher for moderate wasting
  • Impaired immune function
  • Increased susceptibility to infections
  • Potential for permanent developmental damage if prolonged

⚠️ Caution

🚨
Emergency Response Required When:
  • Prevalence ≥ 10% (serious situation)
  • Prevalence ≥ 15% (critical situation)
  • Rapid increase observed (>2% in 3 months)
  • Associated with disease outbreak or food crisis

Treatment and Prevention

💡 Community-Based Management of Acute Malnutrition (CMAM)

  1. Community screening: Using MUAC (Mid-Upper Arm Circumference) for early detection
  2. Outpatient treatment (moderate wasting): Targeted Supplementary Feeding Program (TSFP) with specialized nutritious foods
  3. Outpatient treatment (severe wasting without complications): Outpatient Therapeutic Program (OTP) with Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Foods (RUTF)
  4. Inpatient treatment (severe wasting with medical complications): Stabilization Centers (SC) with F75/F100 therapeutic milk
  5. Prevention: Infant and young child feeding promotion, micronutrient supplementation, seasonal food assistance
Indicator 20: Low Birth Weight

Definition

The proportion of live births with birth weight below 2,500 grams.

Classification

  • Normal birth weight: ≥ 2,500 g
  • Low birth weight (LBW): < 2,500 g
  • Very low birth weight: < 1,500 g
  • Extremely low birth weight: < 1,000 g

🎯
WHO Global Target: 30% reduction in LBW by 2025

Causes of Low Birth Weight

1. Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR):
  • Maternal undernutrition
  • Maternal anemia
  • Maternal infections (malaria, HIV, STIs)
  • Poor antenatal care
  • Adolescent pregnancy (<18 years)
  • Short birth intervals (<24 months)
2. Prematurity (< 37 weeks gestation):
  • Multiple pregnancies (twins, triplets)
  • Maternal illness
  • Obstetric complications
  • Infections

Long-Term Consequences

  • Increased neonatal and infant mortality (20x higher risk)
  • Increased morbidity (infections, hypothermia)
  • Impaired growth and development
  • Higher risk of chronic diseases in adulthood (metabolic syndrome)
  • Intergenerational effects (LBW girls → stunted women → LBW babies)

💡 Interventions to Reduce Low Birth Weight

Antenatal Period:
  1. Early antenatal care enrollment (1st trimester)
  2. Adequate antenatal visits (minimum 8 contacts - WHO recommendation)
  3. Iron-folic acid supplementation (daily for all pregnant women)
  4. Balanced energy-protein supplementation (for undernourished pregnant women)
  5. Prevention and treatment of malaria (ITNs, IPTp)
  6. Prevention of mother-to-child transmission of HIV
  7. Birth spacing counseling (minimum 24 months between pregnancies)
  8. Adolescent pregnancy prevention
Postnatal Period:
  1. Immediate kangaroo mother care for LBW babies
  2. Exclusive breastfeeding support
  3. Infection prevention
  4. Growth monitoring and nutritional support

⚠️
The "Double Burden of Malnutrition" in EAC:
  • Undernutrition remains the primary concern
  • Overweight/obesity emerging problem in urban areas
  • Coexistence possible in same communities, households, even individuals (stunted + overweight)

Risk Factors

  • Urbanization and lifestyle changes
  • Increased consumption of processed foods, sugar-sweetened beverages
  • Reduced physical activity
  • Socioeconomic transitions
  • Aggressive marketing of unhealthy foods to children

  • Type 2 diabetes (emerging in adolescents in urban EAC areas)
  • Hypertension
  • Cardiovascular disease risk
  • Psychosocial problems (bullying, low self-esteem)
  • Continuation into adulthood obesity (80% of obese adolescents become obese adults)

Prevention:
  1. Promote healthy diets and physical activity in schools
  2. Regulate marketing of unhealthy foods to children
  3. Implement sugar-sweetened beverage taxes
  4. Mandatory front-of-package nutrition labeling
  5. Urban planning for physical activity (parks, safe walking/cycling infrastructure)
  6. Comprehensive nutrition education in schools
Treatment:
  1. Family-based behavioral interventions
  2. Individual dietary counseling
  3. Physical activity promotion programs
  4. Psychological support (address emotional eating, body image)

"Having institutional structures is necessary but not sufficient. What truly enables regional monitoring is HARMONIZATION—ensuring that when Kenya measures stunting, and when Tanzania measures stunting, they're measuring it the SAME way, so the numbers can be validly compared and aggregated.

The EAC Framework standardizes across three critical dimensions. First: WHAT to measure—the 23 core indicators with precise definitions. The Harmonization Challenge

The Harmonization Challenge

⚠️ Real Challenge: 2021 Regional Malnutrition Analysis

Dr. Amina Kamara, Regional FNS Analyst (composite of real experiences):

"In 2021, I was tasked with preparing a regional overview of child malnutrition in the EAC. I received data from all countries, but here's what I found:

  • Kenya used WHO 2006 standards for stunting. Uganda was still using NCHS 1977 reference.
  • Tanzania measured children 0-59 months. Rwanda measured 6-59 months.
  • Burundi's data was from 2016 DHS. DRC's was from a 2020 SMART survey covering only 4 provinces.
  • Some countries reported stunting as <-2SD. Others reported moderate (<-2 to -3SD) and severe (<-3SD) separately.
  • Survey sample sizes varied from 1,200 to 15,000 children, affecting precision and comparability.

I couldn't produce a valid regional estimate. The methodologies were too different. That's when I understood: without harmonization, we're flying blind at the regional level."

Three Dimensions of Harmonization

The EAC Framework's Solution: Standardization Across Three Dimensions

Dimension 1: WHAT to Measure
DIMENSION 1: Indicator Definitions (WHAT to measure) 📊
📋 Framework Provision (Chapter 3)

"Partner States should adopt the minimum set of 23 core indicators with standardized definitions and metadata."

Standardization Requirement:

Every Partner State must measure the same 23 indicators using the same definitions. No variations allowed.

Example: Indicator 17 - Prevalence of Stunting

Standardized Definition (Framework Annex 1):

Numerator Number of children aged 0-59 months with height-for-age <-2 SD from WHO 2006 median
Denominator Total number of children aged 0-59 months measured
Unit Percentage

B. Standardized Survey Instruments

📋 Framework Requirement

Partner States shall use EAC-harmonized questionnaire modules for all FNS assessments to ensure data comparability.

Example: Food Consumption Score (FCS) Module

All Partner States must use the identical FCS module:

  • Same 7-day recall period
  • Same food groups (cereals/tubers, pulses, vegetables, fruits, meat/fish, milk, sugar, oil)
  • Same frequency question wording: "In the past 7 days, on how many days did your household consume [food group]?"
  • Same coding protocol (0-7 days)
  • Same weights (staples=2, pulses=3, vegetables=1, fruits=1, meat=4, milk=4, sugar=0.5, oil=0.5)
  • Same thresholds (Poor: 0-28, Borderline: 28.5-42, Acceptable: >42)

Translation Requirement: Questionnaires translated to local languages but back-translated and validated to ensure equivalence. A household answering in Kiswahili in Tanzania should understand the question identically to one answering in Kinyarwanda in Rwanda.

C. Sampling Protocols

📋 Framework Requirement

Surveys must be nationally representative with adequate sample sizes for:

  1. National estimates
  2. Urban/rural disaggregation
  3. Regional/provincial estimates (for large surveys)
  4. Key vulnerable groups (refugees, pastoralists, slums where relevant)

Standardized Sampling Approach:

For Household Surveys (DHS, Nutrition Surveys, Budget Surveys):

  • Design: Stratified two-stage cluster sampling
  • Sampling Frame: Most recent population census enumeration areas
  • Sample Size: Determined using standardized formula
  • Non-response adjustment: Add 10% to account for non-response
  • Cluster size: 20-30 households per cluster (for cost-efficiency)

All Partner States would use this same calculation approach, ensuring comparable precision.

D. Data Quality Assurance Protocols

✓ Framework Mandates

Standardized quality assurance at three stages:

BEFORE Data Collection:

  • Enumerator training: Minimum 5-7 days for comprehensive surveys, 2-3 days for rapid
  • Standardization tests: All anthropometric measurers must pass standardization (WHO criteria: Height TEM <7mm, Weight TEM <100g, MUAC TEM <2mm). No exceptions. Those who fail cannot participate.
  • Pre-testing: Questionnaires tested in 30-50 households, revised based on findings
  • Equipment calibration: Scales, height boards, HemoCue devices calibrated and certified

DURING Data Collection:

  • Daily data review by supervisors (completeness, consistency, outliers)
  • 10% re-measurement by supervisors (spot-check quality)
  • Weekly team meetings to address emerging issues
  • Real-time data transmission (where feasible) for immediate quality feedback

AFTER Data Collection:

  • Data cleaning following standardized protocols (range checks, logic checks, duplicate detection)
  • Plausibility assessment using WHO/SMART criteria for anthropometry
  • External data quality assessment by independent expert before finalization
  • Data quality report published alongside survey report (transparency)

Countries must achieve minimum "GOOD" data quality score for data to be accepted into regional aggregates. Problematic data quality triggers investigation and potential exclusion from regional reporting until issues resolved.

The Challenge:

Malnutrition (especially wasting) is seasonal. If Kenya surveys in March (lean season) and Rwanda surveys in October (harvest), their wasting rates won't be comparable—they're measuring different seasonal realities.

ℹ️ Rationale for July-September Window

This period balances:

  • Post-harvest relative food security (not at seasonal peak of acute malnutrition)
  • Accessibility (after long rains, before short rains in bimodal areas)
  • Comparability (all countries surveying in similar season)
  • Reporting timeline (allows 3-4 months for data cleaning, analysis, report writing to meet Dec 31 deadline)
